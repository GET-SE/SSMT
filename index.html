Towards Engineering a Universal Translator of Spoken Languages

Babel Fish Concept

Science fiction has long imagined universal translators that allow real-time speech-to-speech translation between any two languages. While services like Google Translate can translate text between over 100 languages with reasonable accuracy, we do not yet have the capability for accurate real-time voice translation.

Developing a "Babel Fish" style universal speech translator could be a Grand Engineering Challenge over the next decade. This concept was illustrated in Douglas Adams' Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy with a fictional creature that translates alien languages in real-time when inserted in the ear.

The BBC created a video demonstrating this idea, though the technology does not yet exist. Advances in artificial intelligence and speech recognition may one day make a true universal translator possible.

Speech-to-Speech Translator Problem

Currently, there are limited systems that can translate spoken languages, making Speech-to-Speech Translation a challenging task. It involves recognizing speech at the front-end and synthesizing speech at the back-end, dealing with non-grammatical text. Creating a BabelFish-like device for this purpose could greatly benefit society, especially those who are illiterate. It has the potential to make a significant impact.

Over a Billion People at the Bottom of the Pyramid

People that earn less than $2 a day cannot read or write. They can't follow written directions, read newspaper articles, use many features of a smartphone, benefit from knowledge sources like Wikipedia, or use platforms like Amazon as most educated people do. A Voice-Only Intelligent Assistant, like Alexa, can be used to carry out many such tasks.

Voice-Only Intelligent Agents & Demonstration from the 1990s

The video shows a future where anyone, regardless of their ability to read or write, can interact with an intelligent assistant using voice commands. Twenty years after the video's release, Apple introduced Siri, capable of providing information and executing basic commands through spoken language. In 2015, Amazon introduced Alexa, which had an always-on and always-listening omnidirectional feature. However, even after 30 years, some of the functionalities envisioned in the video are still not fully realized.

Siri and Alexa

Siri and Alexa can assist people who cannot read or write with various tasks:

  • Online Buying and Selling: They can help with buying and selling on platforms like Amazon.
  • Bank Account: They can assist in opening a bank account.
  • Answering Questions: They can answer questions like what, who, where, when, how, and why.
  • Access to Information: They provide access to knowledge and information using resources like Wikipedia.
  • Communication: They enable communication in any language, allowing voice or video emails and chats.
  • Entertainment and Education: They facilitate streaming video translation for both entertainment and educational purposes.

In the future, voice assistant apps similar to Alexa will need a universal translator to be accessible worldwide, particularly for illiterate individuals who cannot read and write.

Language Translation

In 1966, the Automatic Language Processing Advisory Committee (ALPAC) assessed a decade-long language translation research effort. Despite high hopes, the results fell short, leading to significant cuts in funding.

During the research, scientists tackled challenges like translating the phrase "Spirit is willing but the flesh is weak" to "Vodka is strong but the meat is rotten" when moving from English to Russian and back. They delved into methods such as Parallel Corpora and Statistical Translation, which eventually paved the way for the birth of Google Translate.

Excitingly, Google Translate made significant strides, supporting a total of 133 languages after the addition of 24 more languages at Google I/O 2022. To enhance accuracy, researchers focused on finding similarities among languages. They bridged the gaps in translation by understanding language structures through extensive monolingual data and mapping languages using limited bilingual data.

This journey from challenges to breakthroughs paints a vivid picture of the fascinating evolution of language translation technology.

Speech Synthesis

Vocoder at Bell Labs: The journey started with the Vocoder at Bell Labs, exploring Formant Synthesis techniques.

Record, Fragment, Concatenate, and Playback: Researchers began by recording speech, breaking it into fragments, joining them together, and then playing them back, paving the way for further developments.

Text-to-Speech Synthesis (TTS): Text-to-Speech Synthesis, abbreviated as TTS, emerged as a breakthrough. One notable example is the Klatt Synthesizer, famously used by Stephen Hawking.

TTS with Stress and Intonation:

Researchers like Alan Black and Kevin Lenzo, along with companies like Cepstral, focused on developing Text-to-Speech systems that incorporated stress and intonation, making the generated speech more lifelike.

NNet-based Synthesis:

The introduction of Neural Network (NNet)-based synthesis techniques marked a significant leap forward in creating natural-sounding speech patterns.

Text-to-Speech — Lifelike Speech Synthesis 2021:

In 2021, advancements led to the development of even more lifelike speech synthesis techniques, bringing a new level of realism to artificial speech.

Using AI for Societal Good by Empowering Billions at The Bottom of the Pyramid

For over a billion people who cannot read or write, speech is their primary mode of communication. Recent advancements in Speech Recognition, Language Translation, and Speech Synthesis have paved the way for transformative technologies. Imagine a world where people at the Bottom of the Pyramid can use Alexa-like devices, relying solely on voice interfaces. These devices enable:

  • Global Communication: Talking to anyone, anywhere, in any language.
  • Entertainment and Education: Accessing diverse content for learning and entertainment.
  • Economic Activities: Engaging in buying, selling, and banking effortlessly.

Enabling everyone to read any book, watch any movie, and talk to anyone, anywhere in the world is now a possibility. The next grand challenge in engineering is creating a Universal Translator of Spoken Languages, akin to a BabelFish, to make this vision a reality.
